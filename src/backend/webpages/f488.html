Japan stocks give up early gains; Rakuten drops

LOS ANGELES (MarketWatch) -- Japanese stocks opened higher Monday, only to shimmy back down the chart in early trade. The Nikkei Stock Average traded flat, as did the broader Topix, after each opened 0.3% higher, while the dollar bought 105.14, a bit weaker than its 105.38 level at Friday's stock close in Tokyo. Leading Japanese online retailer Rakuten Inc. dropped 3.9% as The Wall Street Journal reported it had reached a deal to buy U.S. shopping-discount site Ebates for just under $100 million. On the other hand, Softbank Corp. rallied 2.3% on excitement surrounding the upcoming U.S. IPO of Alibaba Group, of which it owns roughly one-third. The crucial tech sector saw some early gains, shrugging off the slightly stronger currency and a downward revision to the April-June gross domestic product, announced just before the open. Toshiba Corp. rose 0.8%, Fuji Electric Co. added 1.1%, and Apple Inc. supplier Japan Display Inc. improved by 1.4% ahead of the U.S. company's product-launch event Tuesday. However, Panasonic Corp. slipped 0.4% after strong gains the previous week on announcements tied to its partnership with Telsa Motors Inc. on the latter's "gigafactory." Nintendo Co. fell 2.5% as the Nikkei business daily wrote late last week about the videogame producer's lack of mobile offerings. Meanwhile, stock in Kobe Steel Ltd. enjoyed a 2.3% gain as another Nikkei report said the company plans to join with Chinese partners to produce "extremely light, ultrahigh-tensile steel plate." 8:36 p.m. Sept. 7, 2014

Fed's Rosengren says August job report was disappointing

WASHINGTON (MarketWatch) -- The August job report was "somewhat disappointing," said Eric Rosengren, the president of the Boston Fed, on Friday. His remarks were the first comment on the data from a Fed official. The central bank will go quiet next week as it prepares for its Sept. 16-17 policy meeting. Rosengren, who is on the dovish wing of the committee, is not a voting member this year. In a speech prepared for a conference of bankers in Boston, Rosengren said the U.S. central bank needs to remain patient on rate hikes to give the labor market more time to heal. There is also no upward pressure on inflation coming from wages, he noted. In his speech, he also called for the Fed to move away from any "date-based" forward guidance, and instead focus on "what incoming data tell us" about progress toward the central bank's twin goals of full employment and stable inflation. With the Fed getting close to its goals, and given uncertainty around forecasts, incoming data could cause the central bank to shift the first rate hike forward or backward in time, he said. Rosengren repeated he does not favor a rate hike until the Fed is within one year of achieving its goals of full employment, which he defined as a 5.25% unemployment rate, and inflation close to 2% annual rate. 3:49 p.m. Sept. 5, 2014
