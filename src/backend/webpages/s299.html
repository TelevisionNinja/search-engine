Try as we might, summer is ending. Football season has started. Leaves are at the brink of changing color. And with the passing of Labor Day, school is back in nationwide. Kids and parents are getting back into the swing of thiings. That prompted our weekly look at the interesting and unusual in MLB to look at a different kind of swing.

• This season has seen 150 hitters record four strikeouts in a game, including eight this week. That leaves us four shy of the all-time record for such a thing, set way back in... well, last year. The last eight seasons have been the most prolific in history; only one prior season (1997) even saw 100 such games, and for comparison's sake, there were 82 in the entire decade of the 1940s (albeit with only 16 teams).

• Arizona Diamondbacks teammates Jake Lamb and Nolan Reimold each had four strikeouts in Monday's loss to the San Diego Padres. They're the fourth pair of teammates this season to do it in the same game, but the first in franchise history.

Will Middlebrooks and Allen Craig each had a four-strikeout game for the Boston Red Sox in last week's series at Yankee Stadium. That makes ten 4-K games this season for Boston, most in the American League (the Marlins have 12).

It's the first time in their history that the Red Sox have had two players whiff four times in the same series against their New York rivals; in fact, only once before (2012) had two Bostonians done it against the Yankees in a season.

• Chicago Cubs rookie Javier Baez collected four strikeouts against the Pittsburgh Pirates in Friday's suspended game, already his fifth career four-strikeout game (out of 32 total). The only player in the live-ball era to even have three 4-K games within his first 32 was a pitcher, Johnny Broaca of the Yankees, in 1934; Baez had three more strikeouts on both Saturday and Sunday, becoming the first Cubs hitter with three straight "hat tricks" since Adolfo Phillips in 1966.

The five 4-K games are tied for the major-league "lead" with George Springer and Giancarlo Stanton, and it matches the Cubs "record" (Corey Patterson in 2004). Baez and Springer are the first to have five in their rookie season since Bo Jackson in 1987.

With three weeks remaining, it seems likely that at least one of them will do it again; no player has had six 4-K games in a season since Phillies All-Star Dick Allen did it seven times in the "Year of the Pitcher", 1968.

• In Tuesday's tilt between the Detroit Tigers and Cleveland Indians, starting pitchers Kyle Lobstein and Carlos Carrasco each went 5S innings and needed around 100 pitches just to get that far. Both pitchers rung up 10 opponents, the fourth game this season where both starters hit double digits, but according to Elias, it's the first game since 1900 where both starting pitchers recorded at least 10 strikeouts without either pitching at least six innings.

Because of those strikeouts, Carrasco surrendered just one run despite allowing 10 hits. The last Cleveland pitcher to allow 10 hits but only one run was CC Sabathia in 2006.

However, their last pitcher to contain the damage that well and not get rewarded with a victory was Hall of Famer Hoyt Wilhelm, who pitched the first 11 innings of a game with the Red Sox on May 4, 1958, before Steve Ridzik came on and gave up a walk-off in the 12th.

• David Price, acquired by the Tigers at the deadline, currently leads the majors with 243 strikeouts. Although teammate Max Scherzer and Cleveland's Corey Kluber are still in the running, Price could be the first player since Bert Blyleven in 1985 to lead a league in strikeouts while playing for two different teams. Blyleven, however, only led the AL; Dwight Gooden dwarfed his total that year.

The last multi-team pitcher to lead the majors was Hugh Daily in 1884. Playing in the short-lived Union Association, Daily fanned 469 opponents for the Chicago Browns, who moved at mid-season and became the Pittsburgh Stogies before disbanding. Daily was then picked up for two games (14 strikeouts) by the Washington franchise (also called the Nationals).

• Strikeouts weren't for everyone, however. Madison Bumgarner of the San Francisco Giants technically threw a "quality start" on Saturday with six innings and three runs, but he also allowed 10 hits and struck out zero. It was the first of his 148 career pitching appearances (starter or reliever) where he failed to record a single strikeout.

• In non-strikeout news, the Tampa Bay Rays recorded the first "passed-ball-off" in franchise history on Saturday to beat the Baltimore Orioles 3-2. It was also the first known loss via passed-ball-off in Orioles history. Andrew Miller, who threw only two pitches, became the first Orioles (or Browns) pitcher to end the game on the mound, without recording an out or officially facing a batter, since June 4, 1929. Chad Kimsey, in his third career appearance, entered a tie game in the bottom of the ninth against the Senators, and runner Ossie Bluege promptly stole home with the winning run.

BOSTON -- With the Portland Sea Dogs dropping Game 5 of their Double-A Eastern League semifinal series against the Binghamton Mets 8-5 on Sunday afternoon, Red Sox outfielder Rusney Castillo will join Triple-A Pawtucket for the International League finals starting Tuesday at McCoy Stadium.

Castillo played a full nine innings for the first time in Sunday’s elimination game, going 2-for-5 with a double, a run scored and an RBI while batting leadoff as Portland’s designated hitter.

“I don’t think it’s level of competition. That’s where it was deemed he would go,” manager John Farrell said before Sunday’s game. “If Portland does not advance, he’ll certainly move to Pawtucket to get every available at-bat prior to coming to us. I don't know that I would look too deep into the level, one being more favorable than the other.”

In four games with the Sea Dogs, Castillo went 5-for-14 with four runs scored and two RBIs. He struck out only once -- in his final trip to the plate on Sunday.

Castillo was kept off base Saturday for the only time in his six minor league games.

“Despite going 0-fer yesterday, it’s a matter of him getting his timing down,” Farrell said. “The reports have been favorable, just with the overall explosiveness, the speed, the bat speed. But still, we’re looking at roughly 14, 15 at-bats I believe. Positive, but he’s just getting back into game shape.”

“I’ve seen [video] clips. I’ve not actually gotten game footage to download or to go in depth yet. I fully trust the people that have seen him and obviously to make the investment we have, I think there’ll be a time,” Farrell said. “I don't want to make too much out of the number of at-bats he’s had already. But, just talking to those who have been in the ballpark during games played, [he] looks to be an exciting player.”

Castillo is still expected to make his major league debut this season.

“He just needs at-bats,” Farrell said. “The plan is at some point for him to come to us.”

BOSTON -- Although we didn’t expect a duel between two knuckleballers in the final game between the Toronto Blue Jays and Boston Red Sox this season, one emerged Sunday afternoon at Fenway Park.

Jared Wickerham/Getty ImagesSteven Wright faced just one batter over the minimum in five scoreless innings Sunday.
Toronto had its ace, R.A. Dickey, on the mound to start the game. And after four not-so-impressive innings from Red Sox starter Rubby De La Rosa, Boston countered with its knuckleballer, Steven Wright, who threw five scoreless innings in relief as the Red Sox offense failed to get anything going against Dickey and lost 3-1.

The Sox and Jays are the only two teams in the majors with active knuckleballers on their rosters.

“[Dickey’s] been good against us all year,” Red Sox manager John Farrell said. “We had a couple of opportunities with multiple men on with no outs but he was able to get either a pop up, a strikeout [or] a ball on the ground.”

The much more high profile of the two with a 2012 National League Cy Young to his credit, Dickey earned every bit of his 12th win this season by throwing seven-plus innings and allowing only one run on six hits. However, Wright was equally impressive coming out of the Red Sox bullpen. In his five innings of work he only faced one batter over the minimum, allowing two hits (both singles) and striking out six, the most by a Red Sox reliever since Justin Germano fanned seven Yankees in July of 2012.

Not to mention, Wright also added a pickoff of Jose Reyes at first in the seventh inning, the first of his career.

“It felt good to get out there,” Wright said. “Just trying to come in and throw as many strikes as I could. Just hoping to put it in play with some weak contact so I can try to go as deep as I can.”

Making his first appearance since Aug. 27 when he was still with Triple-A Pawtucket, Wright had no problem shaking off any rust. He utilized three different types of knuckleballs, one fast, one regular and a slower option that he’s tried to mix in more often this season, to keep Toronto off balance and amass six groundouts compared to no flyouts.

“It’s just like a fastball and a changeup but for me it’s a knuckleball,” Wright said of his slow knuckler. “Even if they don’t take it, it kind of disrupts their timing maybe enough to where when I throw my regular or harder one it gets in on them a little bit.”

Although they were unable to adjust to Wright, the Blue Jays successfully did so against De La Rosa, who retired the first six batters he faced before running into trouble in each of his next three innings. Despite racking up five hits between the third and fourth, the Blue Jays were only able to get to De La Rosa in the fifth as Jose Bautista launched a three-run home run to account for their entire offense and knock him out of the game.

“I thought prior to the fifth inning he used all his pitches well,” Farrell said. “He pitched out of a couple of jams. Continued to show very good poise. The 2-0 slider to Bautista unfortunately nicks him for three runs.”

And if it weren’t for Wright, the damage could have been much worse.

“My role is long relief when the starters don’t get an opportunity to go deep,” Wright said. “I’m supposed to eat up some innings. Obviously everybody wants to play but I’m happy to be here and happy to go in whenever I’m needed.”

“Steven Wright came in and did an excellent job for five innings,” Farrell said. “On a day when we can use some length he certainly provided it.”

BOSTON -- While you were most likely watching football Sunday afternoon (and we don’t blame you for that), the Red Sox failed to complete a three-game sweep of the Toronto Blue Jays, losing 3-1 in front of a sellout crowd of 36,261.

The loss was Boston’s 41st at home this season, denying them a winning record at Fenway for just the second time since 1998 (2012 being the other such occasion).

Although he retired the first six batters he faced, starter Rubby De La Rosa danced in and out of trouble in the third and fourth before the Blue Jays finally got to him in the fifth. De La Rosa started the frame with a leadoff walk to Toronto’s No. 9 hitter Anthony Gose, who moved up to third after Jackie Bradley Jr. misplayed a Jose Reyes single to center for his first error of the season. One batter later, Jose Bautista launched his 31st home run of the season to account for all three of Toronto’s runs on the day.

The home run ended the afternoon for De La Rosa, who went four-plus innings allowing three runs on seven hits to tie his shortest start of the season.

De La Rosa’s exit paved the way for a battle of knuckleballers, as Steven Wright came out of the pen in relief the rest of the way to oppose Toronto’s starter R.A. Dickey until his exit in the top of the eighth. Although Dickey earned his 12th win of the season with seven-plus innings of one-run ball, Wright was equally as impressive, giving up two singles over five innings pitched and striking out six. He also added in a pickoff of Jose Reyes at first in the seventh inning, the first of his career.

Wright faced one batter over the minimum in his five innings and has now allowed just one run this season over nine innings pitched.

The Red Sox offense was held to six hits on the day, scoring only in the sixth as Daniel Nava led the inning off with a double and came around on a Xander Bogaerts single to give him his fifth RBI in September, already one more than he had in all of August.

Mookie Betts (2-for-4) doubled to lead off the eighth, chasing Dickey, and stole third with two outs but was left stranded there as the Red Sox couldn’t drive him in against Toronto reliever Brett Cecil.

Despite going 0-for-3, Bradley showed signs of life offensively in his first start since being recalled from Triple-A Pawtucket on Friday. He flew out to deep center in both his first and third at-bat while his second at-bat saw him work Dickey for 11 pitches, including a foul ball to right that had home run distance, before popping out.

Up next: The Red Sox will welcome the AL East division-leading Baltimore Orioles to Fenway for a three-game series. Joe Kelly (3-3, 4.13 ERA) will face off against Miguel Gonzalez (8-7, 3.38 ERA) with first pitch scheduled for 7:10 p.m. on Monday night.

BOSTON -- Being 19 1/2 games out of first place and two games away from division title elimination entering Sunday, the Red Sox are taking no chances in resting some of their ailing players.

David Ortiz is getting a day down as he continues to manage discomfort in his right foot that his plagued him this season. Meanwhile, Brock Holt and Mike Napoli are off as well as both are battling illnesses.

Holt left Friday’s game as a result of dizziness while Napoli was removed from Saturday’s game in the third inning after feeling light-headed and nauseous.

“Mike Napoli feels a little bit better this morning,” manager John Farrell said. “Still going to require another day. Same with Brock Holt, who’s showing some signs of improvement. Hopefully this isn’t something that’s contagious and runs rampant. Both guys are a little bit better today.”

As for Ortiz, Sunday’s game being the 10th straight in a stretch of 17 games over 17 days gives the team a perfect opportunity to rest their 38-year-old slugger. Having fouled a few balls off himself in recent days hasn’t helped, nor did coming around from first to score on a Yoenis Cespedes double in the third inning of Saturday’s game.

“I can’t say he reaggravated it, but knowing that it’s in there we’ve got to be a little bit careful with it,” Farrell said.

Mookie Betts will once again lead things off for Boston, a spot in which he went he went 1-for-4 with a run scored on Saturday. Allen Craig will slide from right field to first as Betts will handle right and Jackie Bradley Jr. will patrol center in his first start since being recalled from Triple-A Pawtucket on Friday. Bradley appeared as a defensive replacement in center in Saturday’s game as Farrell shifted Betts to right to start the ninth.

“I still go by the higher number of balls are going to be in center field. I think we’d be hard-pressed to find a defender better than Jackie Bradley in center field, whether it’s in our uniform or anybody’s uniform, in the major leagues. I think he’s the best center fielder in the game,” Farrell said. “That’s a position that he’s comfortable with. Mookie, while he’s got just a handful games in right field, I feel like he’s athletic enough to move over to right field for a short stint, or at least just today. That’s the thinking behind it.”

Farrell reinforced his praise of Bradley in center by mentioning the outfielder as a legitimate Gold Glove candidate in the American League despite his tumultuous season offensively.

“As someone who votes on those, I don’t know how others might look at the break in service time,” Farrell said. “He’d get my vote from what we’ve seen.”

Too bad managers can’t vote for their own players.

Mujica earns save, auditions for 2015

BOSTON -- For Red Sox pitcher Edward Mujica on Saturday night, the excitement he showed on the field after getting the last out of the game said it all.

A day after being named the team's closer with Koji Uehara struggling mightily over the past several weeks, Mujica's first shot at a save opportunity in his new role came Saturday against the Toronto Blue Jays with a runner on first and one out in a 4-3 game. Mujica was able to preserve the lead, sandwiching a walk between two outs and showing off an emphatic fist pump as he nailed down his first save since Aug. 13.

[+] Enlarge
AP Photo/Winslow TownsonIn his first save opportunity since replacing Koji Uehara as closer, Edward Mujica got it done, finishing off the Blue Jays.
"It's good," Mujica said of the save. "When I signed here they told me, 'You're going to be in the back of the bullpen backing up Koji.' They gave me the opportunity earlier in the season and now I'm just trying to pick up Koji because he's been having rough outings."

For now, Mujica is no longer just a backup to Uehara. Although Farrell said Friday that ninth-inning duties will most likely be handed back to Uehara before season's end, Mujica finds himself in strong position to prove he can be the go-to guy.

And don't let his overall numbers this season fool you. As bloated as his 4.00 ERA may seem for a closer-type, Mujica has pitched much more effectively since a horrifying April that saw him give up 10 runs on 14 hits in nine innings pitched (10.00 ERA).

In fact, he's been dominant at times over the last five months, putting together a streak of 14 straight outings without allowing an earned run from July 27 to Aug. 22 and posting an ERA of 2.78 in 45 1/3 innings since the start of May.

"When I got here they told me they don't want to use me a lot because I pitched a lot last season [with St. Louis]," Mujica said. "Spring training, I think I got like five, six innings only of work. I think April was like tricky for me because I think I need a little bit more work in spring training to get ready for the season. Things can happen, I'm just trying to work hard every single day and get ready."

On Saturday night, Mujica had extra time to get ready for his appearance as Red Sox manager John Farrell stuck with left-handed reliever Drake Britton entering the ninth with Toronto lefties Colby Rasmus and Munenori Kawasaki due up. Britton was able to punch out Rasmus swinging on a 3-2 curveball before losing Kawasaki on ball four and turning things over to Mujica to finish the job. The outing was Britton's second in two days since rejoining the team Friday.

"I'd like to see him continue what he's doing," Farrell said. "This year in Pawtucket might not have been his strongest year but don't lose sight of what he did for us last year at the major league level. He's been durable, he's resilient, he's got good stuff. Any opportunity anybody has here for us is going to be an evaluation period for anyone."

Just as general manager Ben Cherington and Farrell said at the trading deadline, the rest of this season primarily remains an evaluation period for the Red Sox. Much has been made about the influx of new faces into the starting rotation after the departure of Jon Lester, John Lackey and Jake Peavy, while the offense is starting to thrive with the likes of Yoenis Cespedes, Mookie Betts and a reinvigorated Xander Bogaerts.

But don't be so quick to overlook the bullpen. With Craig Breslow, who gave up a two-run home run to Rasmus in the seventh inning on Saturday, continuing to struggle and the Sox holding a team option on him for 2015, seemingly every role in the pen is up for grabs. There's still much to be proven among the Red Sox relief corps the rest of this month.

No matter what happens the rest of the way, Uehara is a free agent after this season. So for Mujica, who's under guaranteed contract with the Red Sox for 2015, the pressure is on to perform well.

"The opportunity is there," Mujica said. "This is a big opportunity for me to go out there and show what I can do."

Would he consider it an audition for the 2015 closer role?

"I don't know,' Mujica said. "They [make] that decision. I have to go out there and just work."

Buchholz bolstering confidence for 2015

BOSTON -- When it became evident the 2014 Boston Red Sox season would be a disappointment in terms of wins and losses, the club began to focus on 2015.

Although the Sox still want to win as many games as possible, individual goals are more important than team accomplishments. On the mound, starter Clay Buchholz wants to have a strong finish to the season, and it appears he's on track to enter the offseason feeling good about his recent performances.

[+] Enlarge
AP Photo/Winslow TownsonClay Buchholz won his second straight start as the Red Sox defeated the Blue Jays on Saturday night.
In his past six starts, the right-hander has a 3.22 ERA over 44 2/3 innings and has recorded 37 strikeouts while issuing only 10 walks. On Saturday, he earned his second straight win to help the Red Sox to a 4-3 victory over the Toronto Blue Jays at Fenway Park.

Buchholz worked 6 1/3 innings and allowed two runs on four hits, with two walks and five strikeouts. He tossed 101 pitches (64 strikes) and improved to 7-8 in his 24th start of the season.

"It always feels good to go out and, obviously, win games, but on an individual level, I feel better with command of all my pitches," Buchholz said. "At some point in the game, I'm using each and every one of my pitches in an effective way. Regardless of it being two pitches in one inning to get outs, I'm using them and have confidence in them. It's just sort of working out right now."

He's recorded quality starts in three consecutive appearances and posted a 1.90 ERA with only five earned runs in 23 2/3 innings.

On Saturday, Toronto pushed across a run in the top of the first inning, but Buchholz pitched out of a jam and kept the damage to a minimum.

After Boston tied the game 1-1 in the home half of the first, Buchholz settled in nicely. He allowed a leadoff single to Kevin Pillar in the top of the second, but after that he retired 12 batters in a row.

"I've faced these guys a lot, too. On any given night, it's either they get you, or you get them," Buchholz said. "It's sort of how it's been when I do face them. There's no remedy to them, I don't think, you've got to pitch careful and sometimes careful leads to mistakes."

Meanwhile, the Red Sox scored three more runs en route to a 4-1 lead through five innings. In the top of the sixth, Buchholz pitched into a bit of trouble, when the first two Toronto batters -- Jose Bautista and Edwin Encarnacion -- led off with a double and walk, respectively. Buchholz then induced Adam Lind into a 1-6-3 double play and ended the threat by getting Dioner Navarro to fly out to center.

In that inning, Buchholz was showing a bit of emotion on the mound, which is unusual for him. He said it was more out of frustration because he had been pitching so well.

"It's one of those things when you're out there, you're grinding, and you feel good with everything you're doing," Buchholz said. "When you miss bad with a couple of pitches, yeah, it gets a little frustrating. Overall, everything's feeling pretty good right now."

As Buchholz's pitch count neared triple digits, he hit Danny Valencia with a pitch to lead off the top of the seventh inning. Pillar popped out to second for the first out of the inning, and Red Sox manager John Farrell came out to take the ball from Buchholz after his 101st pitch of the game.

"I felt fine," Buchholz said. "I just missed up with a couple of pitches on Valencia and ended up hitting him with a cutter. I'd rather hit somebody with a cutter than let him hit a home run and leave it in the middle. It wasn't the right spot to hit somebody and put him on base, and it obviously led to the home run and ended up being a real close game."

The start of the game was delayed 1 hour, 12 minutes due to inclement weather, but Buchholz was not affected.

"I've been a part of them a couple of times now," he said. "You've got to check out for a little bit, knowing that it's going to be a while. They usually give you enough time to get ready whenever they say the game will start. You can't just sit in your locker and stare at it. I check out [and] talk with the guys, and whenever they tell me we're good to go, it's back to game mode."

If he can keep this pace going, it could be a major confidence booster for 2015.

BOSTON -- Make that two in a row for the Boston Red Sox.

Thanks to a strong start by Clay Buchholz and some timely hitting, the Red Sox defeated the Toronto Blue Jays 4-3 on Saturday night at Fenway Park.

The start of the game was delayed 1 hour, 12 minutes due to inclement weather, but Buchholz was not affected. He worked 6 1/3 innings and allowed two runs on four hits, with two walks and five strikeouts. He tossed 101 pitches (64 strikes) and improved to 7-8 in his 24th start of the season.

Dustin Pedroia, Will Middlebrooks, Yoenis Cespedes and Christian Vazquez all contributed an RBI to the victory.

Despite Toronto pushing across a run in the top of the first inning, Buchholz pitched out of a jam and kept the damage to a minimum.

After Boston tied the game at 1-1 in the home half of the first, Buchholz settled in nicely. He allowed a leadoff single to Kevin Pillar in the top of the second, but after that he retired 12 batters in a row.

Meanwhile, the Red Sox scored three more runs en route to a 4-1 lead through five innings. In the top of the sixth, Buchholz pitched into a bit of trouble when the first two Toronto batters -- Jose Bautista and Edwin Encarnacion -- led off with a double and walk, respectively. Buchholz then induced Adam Lind into a 1-6-3 double play and ended the threat by getting Dioner Navarro to fly out to center.

As Buchholz's pitch count neared triple digits, he hit Danny Valencia with a pitch to lead off the top of the seventh inning. Pillar popped out to second for the first out of the inning, and Red Sox manager John Farrell came out to take the ball from Buchholz after his 101st pitch of the game.

As Buchholz watched from the dugout, Red Sox reliever Craig Breslow quickly surrendered a two-run homer to pinch-hitter Colby Rasmus, which narrowed Toronto's deficit to one run. That was the only hitter Breslow faced before Burke Badenhop entered and finished the inning with Boston clinging to a 4-3 lead.

Newly appointed Red Sox closer Edward Mujica entered the game with one out and one runner on in the top of the ninth inning. With two outs, he walked Bautista before getting Juan Francisco to ground out to first base to end the game. It was Mujica's fourth save of the season.

Bugged: Red Sox first baseman Mike Napoli was removed in the top of the third inning due to illness. Allen Craig, who started in right field, moved to first, and Daniel Nava was inserted into right field. Brock Holt was removed from Friday's game due to dizziness. Napoli went 1-for-1 with a run scored before he left the game.

BOSTON -- With his speed and ability to get on base, Mookie Betts could one day prove to be the perfect leadoff man for the Boston Red Sox.

The 21-year-old center fielder will lead off Saturday night against the Toronto Blue Jays for only the second time this season. In 31 big league games, he's batting .280 with four homers, 11 RBIs, 19 runs and four stolen bases. Although Betts looks like the prototypical leadoff hitter, Red Sox manager John Farrell said it will still take some time before the rookie establishes himself at the top of the lineup.

"When [Dustin Pedroia] was out [with a concussion] he handled the two-hole fine for the three or four games he was in there. I can't say that there's been one way that he's been exposed. I think it's a credit to his ability to know the strike zone and hit pitches where they're located."

**Rubby De La Rosa will make his 16th start of the season for the Red Sox on Sunday. The right-hander is 4-5 with a 3.89 ERA. "We cut him fairly short last time out even though I thought he was throwing the ball very well," Farrell said. "We're probably going to be a little bit shorter. "He's holding his velocity well. His last time out compared to the two previous, there was better feel and execution of his secondary stuff. While he's getting up in innings that he's not been accustomed to, I still think he's staying relatively strong."

Fantasy football can make a real difference

September, 6, 2014
Sep 6
1:40
PM ET
By Gordon Edes | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0
  • Comments0
  • Email
  • Print
How's this for a tough sell?

Fantasy football: Not just a place to waste all those hours at the workplace, but a vehicle to perform good deeds. A place to make a difference in the lives of countless others.

Not buying it? We'll leave it up to a baseball player, former Red Sox pitcher John Burkett, to tell you how.

[+] Enlarge
John Mottern/Getty ImagesJohn Burkett (right), pictured in 2003 alongside Wally the Green Monster and Manny Ramirez, wants to make fantasy sports more meaningful.
Burkett retired in 2003 after spending the last two seasons of a 15-year career with the Red Sox, posting 25 of his 166 career victories in games for the Sox in those seasons.

Burkett knows something about meaningful wins. He won playoff games for both the Rangers and Braves, and he was the starting pitcher when the Sox rallied to beat Oakland 5-4 in Game 4 of an ALDS series that the Sox would win in five.

Now he wants you to know about MeaningfulWins.com, how it is making his fantasy football league a difference-maker, and how it can do the same for yours.

Burkett, who lives in Fort Worth, Texas, doesn't just dabble in fantasy football. He has been running leagues for years, recruiting such former teammates as Greg Maddux, John Smoltz, Mike Lowell, Chipper Jones, Matt Clement, Kevin Millar and Mike Napoli to be franchise owners.

"I've had leagues with as many as 94 teams," Burkett said, describing salary caps and drafting procedures that Napoli said finally caused him to drop out because they were too complicated.

"I like to keep it simple," Napoli said.

And how many times has Burkett won one of his own leagues?

"Never," he said. "Kind of embarrassing."

The league isn't about splitting big pots at the end of the season, Burkett said. "Nah, it's not high stakes at all," he said. "It's for fun and the competition, basically."

And then it became for more than that, once Rangers assistant general manager Thad Levine reached out to Burkett to tell him about Meaningful Wins, an idea that he and John Ellis, his former roommate at Haverford College, struck upon.

Ellis, who grew up in Concord, Mass., lives in Arlington and runs an optical engineering company, Optics for Hire, that designs optical engineering systems in Belarus and Ukraine. He and Levine have played in the same fantasy football league with former classmates for years. One season, Ellis said, they decided that it would be a good idea to send part of the winners' pool to charity.

That set them to thinking -- what if there was a way to make that part of the universal fantasy league experience, to devise a mechanism that made giving as reflexive as drafting your team.

"According to the Fantasy Sports Trade Association [yes, there is such a thing], there are over 41 million people playing fantasy sports, and they're spending close to $2 billion to do so," Ellis said. "Take even 1 percent of that for charity, and we're talking $20 million."

Thus was born MeaningfulWins.com, which partnered with such charitable organizations as Doctors Without Borders, Operation Smile, Children's Miracle Network, Team Rubicon and PeacePlayers International, among others.

Levine, having heard about Burkett's leagues, reached out to the former pitcher, who enthusiastically embraced the idea.

"Here's the deal," Burkett said. "There are a couple of ways you can do this. You pay your entry fees, and every player in your league chooses a charity they want to play for -- and it can be any charity, not just the ones you mentioned. Whoever wins the league, the prize money goes to the charity of the winner.

"But that's just one way to do it. The way we do it, we keep our entry fees separate. That money gets divided up the way it always has. But then Meaningful Wins sends an email to every player in your league, asks them if they want to make a donation to a charity, which one, and how much. Some players may decide to give nothing at all, another player might give 100 bucks. No one else in the league knows what you've given, but the total amount pledged now becomes a separate pot, and the winner gets to donate that money to the charity of his choice. And everybody who donates gets a tax receipt."

Burkett was one of the first to sign up with Meaningful Wins, and Napoli tweeted out his support. Ellis said their hope is that eventually fantasy players won't have to go to a separate website to participate, that there will be a clickable button on the big fantasy sites such as ESPN and Yahoo! and CBS where players can automatically make a commitment when they first register.

"I just love the idea," said Burkett, who turns 50 in November and is planning to join the over-50 Professional Bowlers tour next spring. "What a great way to raise money for charity."

Vazquez flashes skills with bat and arm

BOSTON -- Perhaps the memo throughout the league on Boston Red Sox catcher Christian Vazquez should now be updated to include just how dangerous the cannon-armed backstop can be with his bat as well.

Sure, he wasn't the walk-off hero in Friday night's 9-8 10th-inning comeback win over the Toronto Blue Jays. Yoenis Cespedes held those honors with a game-ending single to center. However, Vazquez was the mastermind behind a well-placed bunt single earlier in the 10th inning, allowing Boston to load the bases with no outs and keep the pressure on Toronto until things ended with Cespedes four batters later.

With the team having fallen behind 8-6 after Toronto scored two in the top of the 10th, Mookie Betts and Xander Bogaerts each hit singles to bring life back to a Red Sox offense that had squandered a chance to win with two on and no outs in the bottom of the ninth of a 6-6 game. In stepped the No. 9 man Vazquez, looking to move Betts and Bogaerts over 90 feet for the top of the Red Sox lineup.

While taking ball one, however, Vazquez keenly observed how Toronto's second baseman Steve Tolleson and first baseman Juan Francisco (both late-inning substitutes) were positioned in the infield. With both descending upon him at the plate, Vazquez made the decision to push his bunt to the right side with some loft in an attempt to get a base hit instead. The plan worked as Vazquez's bunt glided over Francisco's head and gave him enough time to reach first base in addition to advancing the runners to second and third.

Mission accomplished.

"I was trying to get them over and I saw [the second baseman and first baseman on] the first pitch," Vazquez said. "I was trying to push that ball and it worked."

Observing from first base, Bogaerts said he felt Vazquez knew exactly what he was doing.

"He's an intelligent player so that probably was on purpose," Bogaerts said. "Smart baseball guy."

Smart indeed, as Vazquez once again made a heads-up play from behind the dish, picking Melky Cabrera off first base in the third inning for his fourth pickoff of the season, the most for a Red Sox catcher since Rich Gedman had four in 1985.

Of course, his defensive résumé at the end of the day continues to garner much more attention than what he can do offensively. However, teams might be best served to not be so quick in counting Vazquez out any time he's at the plate, either. He showed just that Friday night.

"It's fun every time we can score a run and enjoy it," Vazquez said.

BOSTON -- It appeared to be a typical game of seasonal bad luck for the Boston Red Sox, but in the end they reached into their pockets and found a bit of 2013's comeback, walk-off magic, finishing with a 9-8 win in 10 innings over the Toronto Blue Jays on Friday night at Fenway Park.

Yoenis Cespedes provided the walk-off heroics with a deep blast to center field with the bases loaded. It was his second career walk-off hit, and first with the Red Sox.

Cespedes continues to be locked in at the plate. He went 4-for-6 with two RBIs and scored a run. He had three singles and a double and also stole a base.

The hot-hitting left fielder came up short with two runners on and no outs with the game tied at 6-6 in the bottom of the ninth when he popped out to second, but he responded in extra innings.

Since joining the Red Sox on Aug. 2, Cespedes has reached base safely in 27 of his 32 games.

Prior to the walk-off, the Red Sox's hopes seemed bleak.

In the top of the 10th inning, Dioner Navarro's broken-bat infield single drove in one run for Toronto, and then a miscue by Red Sox right fielder Allen Craig allowed the Blue Jays to push across another.

After Toronto gained a 7-6 lead on Navarro's one-out hit, Danny Valencia lifted a high fly ball to deep right field. Toronto's John Mayberry stood on second, waiting to tag up. Craig made the catch at the wall in front of the visitors bullpen and seemed to think it was the third out of the inning, taking his time throwing the ball back into the infield. Mayberry was waved home to give Toronto a two-run lead.

But Boston responded in the home half en route to victory.

Red Sox starter Allen Webster settled for a no-decision. The right-hander worked 5 1/3 innings and allowed four runs (three earned) on six hits, with two walks and five strikeouts. He tossed 91 pitches (60 strikes).

The Blue Jays gained a 2-0 lead in the top of the first inning, thanks to a two-run double by Adam Lind. Boston responded in the home half of the inning when Cespedes provided a two-out, RBI single to cut the Sox's deficit.

Boston tied the game at 2-2 in the bottom of the second inning when Christian Vazquez's sacrifice fly to right field easily scored Mookie Betts from third. The Red Sox gained a 3-2 lead in the bottom of the third inning on Mike Napoli's infield groundout, as Dustin Pedroia scored from third.

But in the top of the sixth inning, with Webster's pitch count climbing, Jose Bautista crushed a two-run homer into the Monster seats to give Toronto a 4-3 lead. It was Bautista's 30th home run of the season.

Toronto held a one-run lead until the top of the eighth inning, when Edwin Encarnacion absolutely crushed a two-run homer off Red Sox reliever Alex Wilson. It was Encarnacion's 30th homer of the season and it cleared everything over the Monster to give Toronto a 6-3 lead.

Again, Boston chipped away at Toronto's lead and once again tied it at 6-6 in the bottom of the eighth inning.

Cespedes led off with a single and Napoli followed with an infield single. Both runners then successfully executed a double steal. With one out, Betts provided an RBI single, before Xander Bogaerts drove a two-run double to right-center field to tie the game at 6-6.

In the bottom of the ninth, Pedroia led off with a double to left. The Blue Jays elected to intentionally walk Ortiz, before Cespedes popped out to second base in shallow center field. With one out and two on, Napoli struck out swinging. The Red Sox left two stranded as Craig grounded out to the pitcher to end the threat.

After Toronto pushed across a pair of runs in the top of the 10th inning, the Red Sox responded with their heroics in the bottom of the inning en route to victory.

He's back: After missing five games with a concussion, Pedroia returned to the lineup and went 2-for-5, including a single, a double, run scored and sacrifice fly.

Don't test me: Opponents have the scouting report on Vazquez's strong and accurate arm, which makes them think twice about running on the Red Sox catcher. Now, it's getting to a point where opposing baserunners can't even take a lead, especially off first base. In the top of the third inning, Melky Cabrera drew a one-out walk and was taking his customary lead off the bag when Vazquez made a quick snap throw to pick off Cabrera. It's the fourth time this season Vazquez has picked off a runner. It's the most by a Red Sox catcher since Rich Gedman had five in 1985.

Web gem: With one out and the game tied at 6-6 in the top of the ninth inning, Kevin Pillar turned on an offering from new Red Sox closer Edward Mujica and sharply lined it toward third. Brock Holt showcased his quick reflexes and snared the ball for the second out of the inning. Holt left the game in the top of the 10th inning due to illness and was replaced by Will Middlebrooks.
