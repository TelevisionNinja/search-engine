Jets show some promise, no polish

With the Pack up next, Geno and the Jets had better clean up Sunday's messy win

By Johnette Howard | ESPNNewYork.com

NEW YORK -- Second-year Jets starter Geno Smith looked like a marginally better quarterback than he was a year ago. But even he agreed Sunday that low standard shouldn't be the yardstick for him anymore, and whatever progress he has made with his reads or command of the huddle might not matter, anyway -- not if he keeps turning over the ball and playing behind a self-sabotaging offense that showed some promise, but not a lot of polish, in the Jets' season opener against Oakland.

The Jets should've run the lousy Raiders off the field. Instead, they had to settle for a 19-14 win at MetLife Stadium after they survived 11 penalties, a handful of bonehead decisions, a dropped touchdown pass and a dropped interception that earned the team a rare public spanking from head coach Rex Ryan.

"This should have been a beatdown ... a rat kill," Ryan said in reference to how the Jets outgained the Raiders 402 yards to 158. "It got a bit too close for comfort there."

Except for a little fluffery about what could be for Smith -- "The kid can throw it when the protection holds up. He can make all the throws." -- Ryan even let Smith have it a little, along with the rest of the offense. And Ryan should have.

The Jets travel to Green Bay next to play a Packers team that was embarrassed in its season opener in Seattle.

[+] Enlarge
Brad Penner/USA TODAY SportsRex Ryan, left, came down on Geno Smith and the offense after a sloppy Sunday, and rightfully so.

Oakland's rookie quarterback, Derek Carr, couldn't make the Jets pay for the error-strewn game Smith and the offense hung up, the scoring attempts they squandered and the offense's part in committing seven of those 11 penalties, three of them momentum-killers by veteran guard Willie Colon. (An eighth penalty for offensive holding was declined because Smith was sacked anyway.) But the Jets won't beat Aaron Rodgers and the Packers -- or survive the Bears, Lions, Chargers, Broncos and Patriots that come right after that -- if they're not sharper.

Their season could get very ugly very quickly.

"We have to set the bar higher for ourselves," Smith said. "And it starts with me."

Smith's statistical line looks fine at first glance -- 23-of-28 passing for 221 yards and a shovel pass touchdown -- if you didn't happen to see the first-quarter interception he threw at the Jets' 24, which set up the Raiders' first touchdown. Or the second-quarter fumble he coughed up after getting clobbered by two Oakland defenders near the Raiders' goal line. Or the back-back-backpedaling sack he took for a 19-yard loss to kill yet another promising drive in the fourth quarter. He had a second fumble, but he recovered that one.

"Big mistakes," Ryan said.

Referring specifically to the sack Smith took when the score was still an uncomfortably close 13-7, the Jets, Ryan added, "Know the down and distance. We're in field-goal range. Either take the sack, or throw it away."

Smith was equally straightforward about how he played. He knew there was no excuse for that play or his interception. He said he saw the Raiders' Charles Woodson but admitted he misjudged the window he had to throw the ball into and was a tick slow getting the ball there.

But Smith should only share the blame for his first fumble with whomever thought it was a bright idea to bring backup quarterback Michael Vick into the game for that series as a decoy and run a gadget play that left Smith running that option keeper to the right. He was absolutely crumpled by Raiders linebacker Sio Moore and cornerback TJ Carrie and had the ball stripped away. Then he was very slow getting up.

"He did get hit ... that scared me a little bit," Ryan admitted.

The problem wasn't, as Ryan said afterward, that Smith needs to slide and protect himself on that play. No player just a couple yards from the goal line, not even a quarterback, is going to give up without a fight to get in.

The real problem was the Jets asked Smith to run the play, period. But then, a weird affection for red-zone trickery has been a hallmark of Ryan's coaching tenure. Early on, it used to be blamed on offensive coordinator Brian Schottenheimer because, well, Schottenheimer was blamed for everything, if Mark Sanchez wasn't. But three offensive coordinators later, Rex has to own this one, as well as another red-zone gadget play in which running back Chris Johnson took a direct snap and handed it off to Vick on an end around, only to see Vick overthrow Eric Decker in the end zone.

"It obviously never worked the way we wanted it to," Ryan admitted.

Ryan's tone was never over-the-top angry as he assessed it all. He went on to confess he couldn't remember feeling this bad after a win in which the stats were so lopsided in favor of his team.

Abandoning his usual refusal to criticize his own players was the right tone to strike.

If the Jets can take away the gaffes, Marty Morinwheig's offense is imaginative, conceptually. It could be pretty good if, as Ryan also groused, "This team didn't keep shooting itself in the foot." Decker had a solid debut (five catches, 72 yards), despite two penalties committed. The Jets' running-back tandem of Chris Johnson and Chris Ivory (71-yard touchdown run) combined for 170 yards and two touchdowns.

Even Smith did a lot of things well. He threw the ball both deep and short with encouraging accuracy. He stood up to the rush, slid around in the pocket well and generally made good decisions about when to take off and run. He even put his shoulder down on one occasion to get a first down.

He seemed to see the field well, but it was still barely good enough to survive against a lousy Oakland team Sunday.

And it won't be good enough many game days after this.

The real season starts for the Jets next Sunday.

"And it has to get better ... It has to get better ... We have to get better," Ryan said.
