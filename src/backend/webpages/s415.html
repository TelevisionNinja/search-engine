Recap: No. 10 Prep 23, No. 6 B-R 12

September, 6, 2014
Sep 6
8:16
PM ET
By Scott Barboza | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0
  • Comments0

BRIDGEWATER, Mass. – St. John’s Prep’s first half offensively was characterized by missed opportunities. Three times during the first 22 minutes of their road contest at No. 6 Bridgewater-Raynham, the 10th-ranked Eagles were flagged with penalties while in the red zone.

But Prep figured things out in time, scoring 20 unanswered points to close out a 23-12 win over the Trojans, avenging a home loss to B-R one year ago. The victory was the first for new head coach and alumnus Brian St. Pierre, who was award the game ball by his team in their postgame huddle.

“I was chomping at the bit,” said the eight-season NFL veteran. “I got a little animated during the pregame speech.”

That enthusiasm didn’t translate to early success for St. Pierre’s team, however. B-R (0-1) systematically marched 80 yards downfield on the opening drive of the game, as Trojans quarterback Matt Clement completed all three of his passes to wide receiver Matt Reagan, culminating in a 9-yard touchdown pass. It resulted in an instant 6-0 Trojans’ lead that required only 2 minutes, 25 seconds of game clock.

Prep (1-0) also was able to move the ball effectively on its first drive, but the Eagles stalled with a first-and-goal from the 8-yard line. An offensive holding call backed them up to the 18 and, three plays later, Prep was forced to settle for a 36-yard field goal but Justin Foley.

After B-R thwarted a Prep fourth-and-goal bid from the 5, the Trojans took a 12-3 lead midway through the second quarter, when Clement hit Reagan for their second touchdown connection on the day – this time from 56 yards.

The Eagles would again be confronted with a fourth-down decision deep in Trojans’ territory before the half was out. With a fourth-and-six from the B-R 13, St. Pierre remained aggressive.

“I didn’t want to settle for a field goal, we did on the first drive,” he said.

The move paid off as first-time starting quarterback Oliver Eberth hit Owen Rockett for their first of two scores, whittling B-R’s lead to 12-10 at the half.

Eberth (9 of 17, 143 yards) and Rockett (5 catches, 60 yards) resumed play in the second half as they’d left it in the first, as the senior wideout took a simple swing pass upfield for a 35-yard touchdown.

“We were hoping to get the linebacker pinched in a little bit and we hit it just in time,” Rockett said. “From there, it was just making a play.”

Knock, knock, knockin’: Prep ran away with the game in the second half, thanks to a relentless rushing attack, led by senior Cody Harwood. The Eagles rushed for 225 yards on the game, with nearly three-quarters of those yards coming in the second half.

Harwood punched in 23 of his game-high 123 yards on the Eagles’ final score with 7:50 remaining in the game. Junior Dillon Preston (10 carries, 47 yards) also provided tough yards on the ground running behind junior fullback Nick Spofford.

“The whole game they played really well,” Harwood said of Prep’s offensive line. “It was just opening the holes and then it was just me one-on-one with the backer, trying to make the play. They were just worn down that second half.”

Lack of thump: While Saturday’s affair looked to be a test of rushing attacks entering action, B-R was limited in its ability to run the ball consistently.

ESPN Boston preseason Mr. Football candidate Brandon Gallagher left the game in the first quarter due to an ankle sprain. Matters were made worse when Gallagher’s replacement – senior Isaiah Bell (4 carries, 22) was injured while attempting to make a defensive tackle in the second quarter.

With the Trojans’ thunder out of the lineup, the offense was left to Clement primarily. He acquitted himself well, completing his first seven consecutive pass attempts, while completing 9 of 15 attempts for 122 yards.

“We live and die with the play-action pass and the running game,” B-R head coach Dan Buron said. “It wasn’t there, so [Clement] had to work with it. I though he did a nice job considering – it was a good day, not a great day.”

Clement’s comfort with Reagan was apparent, as the junior wide receiver caught five balls for 94 yards.

“He did well, he made some plays and Matty [Clement] found him,” Buron said of Reagan. “So we’re going to be OK. I’m disappointed in the loss, but, in the big picture, I think we improved today.”

A new era: Following the victory, St. Pierre reflected on the legacy former Prep coach and current athletic director Jim O’Leary left with the program. St. Pierre is just the third Prep coach in the last 47 years, following O’Leary (30 seasons) and Fred Glatz (17 seasons).

“When you follow a legend, the bar’s set pretty high,” said St. Pierre, who won a Super Bowl title while quarterbacking the Eagles in 1997. “You feel that. We’ve been a successful program for a long time and you fell that. But this is where I feel I belong. I had a great experience here as a player and as a student.”

Recap: No. 14 Holliston 34, Duxbury 20

September, 6, 2014
Sep 6
3:33
AM ET
By Mike Uva | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0

DUXBURY, Mass. –- Holliston may be known primarily for its brand on spread of offense. But what the Panthers showed tonight in their 34-20 win over Duxbury is that they're not afraid to change the pace.

Tonight, that involved by going to what they call their “war” package, a double-tight formation.

“It was kind of like two heavyweight fighters, just trying to feel each other out in that first round,” explained Holliston coach Todd Kiley. “Once we got a feel for what they were doing, we tried to make adjustments and war is always an option.”

After struggling to move the football in the first quarter, Kiley opted to change the tempo and run "War". On the very first play of the second quarter, Zach Elkinson took the handoff and dashed 80 yards for the score. From there on, the Panthers’ offense caught fire.

Later in the second, on back-to-back series, Nick Athy hooked up with running back Joe Bellomo through the air for two scores. The first coming on a 10-yard pass and the second on a short screen pass that Bellomo turned into a 46-yard scamper, finding the end zone once again.

With momentum quickly getting away from Duxbury, sophomore quarterback Bobby Maimaron led charge, finding senior receiver Brian Zec on a 32-yard pass in the end zone, to get the Dragons on the board, 18-6.

But as soon as Holliston got the ball back for their next offensive series, Bellomo was at it again -– this time on the ground. The 6-foot, 205-pound running back rumbled 46 yards to give the Panthers a 26-6 lead at the half.

Despite allowing 283 yards rushing in the first half, Duxbury fought back allowing 91 yards in the second half. Creating turnovers on defense, the Dragons gave Maimaron and the offense the opportunity to get into a rhythm. Maimaron tossed two more bullets two close the Panther’s lead to 26-20. The first came on a 21-yard rocket finding a leaping Ryan Regan and the second was a short 5-yard curl to Cam Walsh.

Although Duxbury fought their way back behind the three touchdown passes of Maimaron, they had no answer of stopping Holliston when they needed to the most, as Athy put the game out of reach going 31 yards on a run up the middle for the score making it 34-20 with just under two-minutes in the game.

Despite the outcome, Duxbury coach Dave Maimaron was pleased with the way his players fought back in the second half.

“We dug ourselves a hole early,” said Maimaron, whose 92-17 now as coach for the Dragons. “We had plenty of opportunities and if one of those plays go our way it could’ve been a different game. [Holliston] is very good team and with the weapons they have on offense, you can’t fall behind early.”

Workhorse: Bellomo put on a clinic in the season opener, rushing for 178 yards and racking up just over 250 all-purpose yards. The senior captain, who also wrestles at Holliston High School, scored all three of his touchdowns in the first half and was a force to be wrecking with in short yardage situations. While the limelight may have been on him tonight, the humble four-year starting running back turned the attention towards his offensive line for creating the running lanes.

“The blocking was awesome,” Bellomo said with a smile. “They opened up great holes and I was fortunate enough to run through them. The entire line played great, but one play in particular, John DoCarmo came out of no where and decleated a kid that sprung me for one of my scores. It’s a lot of fun being able to run the ball when you know you have guys like that upfront blocking for you.

Controlling the air: Kiley may believe his starting cornerback A.J. Schneeloch can be quite, but when he’s out on the field his performance is loud and clear –- ball hawk. The defensive back was all over the field for the Panthers, having two pass breakups and adding an inception in the end zone that prevented Duxbury of making a run before the half. What impressed coach Kiley the most is just the way he’s able to rise to an occasion knowing that the team is depending on this play on defense.

“A.J. [Schneeloch] had a huge assignment tonight covering Walsh, but he responded, said coach Kiley. “We try to put him on [Duxbury’s] best receiver because he’s got great technique and has the ability to make big plays. I mean that pick he had before the end of the half was a monster play for us; it was huge. I couldn’t be happier about his performance tonight.”

D-Y's Ian Crosby proved ready and able

September, 6, 2014
Sep 6
1:43
AM ET
By Scott Barboza | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0
  • Comments0
  • Email
  • Print


HYANNIS, Mass. – Ian Crosby didn’t expect much from Friday night’s season opener against Barnstable. The Dennis-Yarmouth junior backup quarterback knew his script. He was excited for the opportunity he had after being handed a series – or so – of time commanding the offense, playing a foil to starting quarterback Michael Dunn.

Crosby, who spent the last couple seasons starting for the Dolphins’ junior varsity team, didn’t realize the extent to which he’d determine the outcome of the Cape rivals’ contest, however. After senior starter Michael Dunn was forced from the game early in the third quarter with a leg cramp, the huddle was Crosby’s to run.

“You always have to be prepared for those types of situations,” Crosby said. "I have to give a lot of credit to Coach [Paul] Funk and Coach [Joe] Jamiel for being great coaches and really helping me out, to be ready for those games, being cold and going in and having those big game opportunities.”

Crosby entered the game with gusto, completing his first seven attempts of the game. He finished the game completing 8 of 13 for 63 yards.

But what Crosby provided the offense was more than his stat line. He entered the game on D-Y’s first offensive possession of the second quarter. Lined up in the shotgun in a four-wide set, Crosby’s coolness at quarterback allowed Dolphins coach Paul Funk and staff to push Dunn (a preseason Mr. Football award candidate) to running back. That, in change, allowed the Dolphins a greater number of looks in their read option game, after Dunn and the offense struggled to gain a foothold on the ground against a physical Barnstable defensive front seven.

Ironically, it was the Dolphins’ starting quarterback, whom Crosby replaced, that he credited with much of his success after stepping into his first varsity game.

“He’s a great leader and he’s a great kid to look up to,” Crosby said of Dunn. “Whenever I’m going through practice, going through drills, I’m always looking at his feet to see what I can do to get better. He’s just a great role model for me to get better.”

Crosby admitted that although he was on the money with his throws to start, things looked a bit blurry initially.

“The varsity level is clearly a lot faster,” Crosby chuckled. “But I don’t know, I was just feeling hot tonight. I give a ton of credit to my players, being able to get open and making the work a lot easier on me.”

The next-man-up mentality befits the Dolphins’ offensive mentality, which maximizes its players’ athleticism, using them in across a gamut of roles.

Take, for instance, “half back” (as he’s listed on D-Y’s roster) Andrew Jamiel. Not only was Jamiel the Dolphins’ leading receiver on Friday – making catches both out of the backfield and split out wide – he also deal a dagger to the Red Raiders’ comeback hopes with a 15-yard touchdown run in the fourth quarter, reestablishing a two-possession lead.

“It’s good because you get to get a look from every part of the field,” Jamiel said. “Michael [Dunn], it was great to see him come back, but Ian [Crosby] really did step up. We didn’t expect him to get more than four, five snaps. It was a great job by him.”

For Crosby, Friday’s unexpected prominence in game action was no accident. If he wasn’t ready to perform, he wouldn’t have stuck.

There’s that old adage about putting in the work when no one is working – that’s what gets you noticed.

“It’s going and working hard in the preseason, getting my footing down, working on mechanics,” Crosby said. “I felt like I came out and I was ready for this game.”

Recap: No. 3 Everett 36, No. 17 Springfield Central 15

SPRINGFIELD, Mass. -- When it comes to season openers, all players and coaches are geared up. Yet there is still that feeling of the unknown that dwells in each and everyone of them.

Coming into Friday night's tilt, both Everett and Springfield Central had the usual question marks in terms of how newcomers would replace those who have since graduated. What you see in a normal practice or an obscure scrimmage is not quite the same as an actual live game. The Crimson Tide had some questions concerning their offense answered for at least one evening anyway as they out-scored the Golden Eagles 36-15 at a humid Berte Field.

Everett amassed 372 yards of offense, led by senior back Nick Orekoya, who shredded the Central defense for for 152 yards on 14 carries and a pair of touchdowns.

He was not alone in the Tide's ample running attack. Senior Lukas Denis finished with 67 yards and Luis Cardona, a junior, finished with 42. When the call came to pass the ball, sophomore quarterback Jordan McAfee connected on 7 of 16 passes for 108 yards and a score. Everett also did a nice job with ball control as they were able to put together five drives that covered 50 yards or more.

"Overall we are happy with some things but disappointed with other things," Everett head coach John DiBiaso said. "But its the first game. Generally they say you make your biggest improvements between your first and second game so hopefully that'll be true for both of these teams here tonight. Because of the heat and humidity we were going with three guys in the backfield. We had a bunch a guys going down with cramps. I thought the fact that we had three running backs that we could utilize really helped us."

The Tide led just 15-13 midway through the second quarter before mounting a 64 yard drive capped off by Gary Clark's touchdown reception on a fourth-and-10 play from the Golden Eagles' 13 yard line with just over three minutes left before the half. Putting up some distance between themselves and Central seemed to light a fire under them.

The Golden Eagles came into this season having to replace a stalwart of offensive talent, including 2013 ESPN Boston Offensive Player of the Year Cody Williams, but showed they still had depth at some key positions. Central opened the second half by putting forth a well-executed drive. An Aaron Williams 26-yard touchdown completion to Antoine Williams went for naught after the Golden Eagles were flagged for blocking below the waist. They never quite seemed to recover from that, as the drive stalled on the Tide 13.

Everett got the ball back and marched 87 yards behind the stellar running of Orekoya to set up his own 43 yard jaunt up the middle extending the lead to 29-13.

"As a team I thought we played great," Orekoya said. "The defense came out and did their thing and we have a very good offensive line and I was able to run behind them. The line played great and we have a lot of good running backs on this team."

Central finished with 307 yards of offense, the majority of it coming from junior running back Tyreque Estrada-Crapps (20 carries, 104 yards). Senior Aaron Williams, getting his first legit opportunity as a starting signal-caller, completed 11 of 19 passes for 89 yards.

The loss to Everett marked the fourth in as many years for the Golden Eagles. After losing the season opener to the Tide last year, Central ran off 10 straight victories before falling to St. John's of Shrewsbury in the Division 2 state semifinal contest.

The Golden Eagle offense was limited throughout the second half by a staunch Everett defense which limited them to only 130 yards.

"We played much better defensively in the second half," said DiBiaso. "But we know there are things we need to tighten up and I'm sure (Central coach Valdamar Brower) will tell you the same thing about his team. We were using timeouts because there were times when we had only 10 guys on the field and sometimes 12 guys on the field. We were in some wrong sets as well. So those are things we really need to work on this week."

Early in the final quarter and the game still within reach of Central, all hopes were officially shattered after Aaron Williams was stripped of the ball by linebacker Mark Cardwell inside Everett's red zone. Junior John Uga scooped it up and ran it back 92 yards the other way making it 36-13. Central accounted for a safety late to give them two points.

The Tide wasted no time getting on the board first. On their initial possession, they needed just 55 seconds before Denis bolted 47 yards down the right sideline for a 7-0 lead. The Golden Eagles answered on the ensuing possession. A Crapps 60-yard jaunt was followed up moment later with an 8 yard carry into the end zone. The extra point was missed and Central trailed by one. Everett went up 15-6 on the first play of the second quarter. Driving 71 yards, Orekoya barreled over from the 3 yard line and Cardona followed with the conversion run.

Once again, Central had an answer. A Crapps 47 yard reception set the tone for his 3 yard scoring run with 7:33 remaining before halftime closing the gap at 15-13. But Clark's scoring reception four minutes seized any momentum the Golden Eagles had mustered.

"I think their coaching staff made some good adjustments but I also felt at times we made some good adjustments as well," said Brower. "We played a lot of young guys tonight but many of them did rise up and I was excited in how they performed. We just need to clean some things ups and get stronger.

"The kids stayed together and just kept fighting. Tyreque is a player for us who has great vision and great potential. We just have to keep making him better and also make everyone around get better too so we can become great."

Recap: No. 15 BC High 18, No. 24 Marshfield 13

September, 6, 2014
Sep 6
1:19

By Nate Weitzer | ESPNBoston.com

DORCHESTER, Mass. -– With their team down 13-12, and visiting Marshfield in possession of the football with just over two minutes to play, some BC High students began to file out of James Cotter Field. After all, the Eagles just drove 53 yards to set up a go-ahead field goal attempt late in the fourth quarter, but the notorious swirling winds at BC High’s home stadium coaxed the kick wide.

So even if they managed to get the ball back, the Eagles faithful figured that the offense would essentially have to punch in a TD to come away victorious in their home opener.

But senior quarterback Sean Holleran said that he “never had a doubt.”

BC High forced Marshfield into a three-and-out, wide receiver Will Johnson made several Rams miss on the ensuing punt return, and Holleran came through in the clutch. The 5-foot-11 signal-caller was dynamic on the Eagles final possession, scrambling for a 19-yard gain that resulted in a huge first down, and miraculously escaping Marshfield’s pressure to throw the ball away on the next play. That gave Holleran the opportunity to run a well-desinged play on 2nd down, as he found find Nick Gill in the back of the end zone from 16 yards out, and put his squad up 18-13 with 26 seconds left on the clock.

“The past two seasons we’ve come out and lost our home opener, but we had a shot to get it this time, and we just wanted it.” Holleran said. “The offensive line played great. Even though they were exhausted from playing two ways, they fought through it. Gill made a great move to get open on that post route and really came through for us.”

For Marshfield, it was a crushing blow to what would have been an excellent early addition to their resume. The Eagles are known for their size up front, but it was the Rams that controlled the tempo for most of the final three quarters, as they racked up 113 rushing yards after initially failing behind 6-0.

BC High had no answers for Marshfield’s misdirection running plays. As soon as the Eagles tried to crowd the line of scrimmage, senior Jack Masterson would execute a brilliant play fake. The Rams' quarterback hung in the pocket on one such play, and threw a 40-yard strike to junior WR Jason Darcy despite getting nailed by two defensive linemen. That TD put Marshfield up 13-12 with 10:39 left in the fourth quarter, but it wasn’t enough.

Opening Up The Playbook: With head coach Jon Bartlett back on the sidelines after a two-year hiatus (during which he stayed on as the school’s athletic director), the Eagles appeared to find a balance they had been lacking. BC High was one of the premier defensive teams in Division 1 last season, holding opponents to 11.9 points per game, yet they lacked firepower as they managed to score just 14.4 points per game.

The Rams had the momentum after executing a 90-yard TD drive in the second quarter of this contest, and that’s when Bartlett decided to open up his playbook. BC High had been running a conservative offense to that point, so when backup quarterback Marco King sprinted towards the edge on an end-around, paused, and threw a bomb to a wide-open Nick Gill at the 5-yard line, it caught everyone in the stadium by surprise.

Gill dropped the pass (setting up a great opportunity for redemption later on), but Will Johnson brought the Eagles near the goal line with an incredible 31-yard run after catching a screen pass, and BC finished the drive with an HB pass from Steve Timmins to Johnson in the corner of the end zone.

“We’re capable of opening it up,” Bartlett said.. “We want to see what the defense gives us, and Marshfield has very good linebackers, so the inside run was hard to come by. We felt we had better matchups on the edge, especially with Will (Johnson), who is a heck of a player. Other people might not know about him yet, but they will soon.”

Doing A Little Bit Of Everything: Timmins had quite the first half as a two-way player for BC High. The junior picked off Masterson to end Marshfield’s first drive, and broke a 38-yard run before finishing the frame with a rare TD pass.

Contrasting Styles: While the Eagles hung their hat on defense last season, the Rams ended up in plenty of shootouts, and allowed 40 points or more in four different contests. BC High allowed 11.9 points per game and scored 14.4, while Marshfield scored 28.5 and allowed a whopping 29.5. Those different results may have been partially due to the programs' contrasting styles.

Both of these teams employed a spread offense at times, but they lined up in significantly different formations to start the game. BC High executed seven of their first eight plays out of an I-formation as they marched 61 yards for a TD to start the game, while Marshfield operated almost exclusively out of the shotgun.

Masterson orchestrated a complex rushing scheme as Marshfield increased their pace and got BC High off balance in the second quarter. The Rams ran 13 plays on their 90-yard drive, 11 of which came out of shotgun formations. Despite completing 8 of 11 passes, Masterson wasn’t asked to do too much with his arm. Marshfield’s rushing attack looked unstoppable at times, but head coach Lou Silva’s decision to call three consecutive runs during the Rams final possession may have been a critical mistake.

Recap: No. 12 D-Y 36, No. 23 Barnstable 21

September, 6, 2014

1:13

By Brendan C. Hall | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0
  • Comments0

HYANNIS, Mass. –- First Andrew Jamiel broke up the monotony. Then he broke his archrivals’ backs.

The senior athlete was as slippery as ever tonight for Dennis-Yarmouth in its annual season-opener with Cape Cod rival Barnstable, totaling two touchdowns as the Dolphins came away with a 36-21 win, their fourth over the Red Raiders in five seasons. And those two scores couldn’t have come at more clutch moments.

Late in the second quarter, the Dolphins ahead 14-7 in an otherwise lethargic first half, Jamiel planted his heels at his own five yard line and watched Raiders quarterback Griffin Burke evade the rush. Before he could break on the ball, it landed in his bread basket, overthrown, giving way to what will go down as one of the top plays of the season across the state.

Jamiel strode to his right, reversed 180 degrees to the left sideline, then put on the burners before making one more cut back to the middle of the field roughly 15 yards out, completing a 95-yard interception return that made it 22-7 with a minute to go in the half.

“I was just in the right place at the right time,” Jamiel shrugged.

Then with five minutes to go in the contest, leading 28-21, Jamiel struck again. Given a short field, then a 15-yard unsportsmanlike penalty to set up first and 10 at the Raiders’ 29, the Dolphins called two quarterback keepers for Michael Dunn then hit one of Jamiel’s specialties for paydirt, a 15-yard end around that made it 36-21 following a two-point rush.

“Andrew Jamiel is a gamer,” D-Y head coach Paul Funk said. “He’s a heck of a player…Andrew made things happen. That’s what his M.O. is, he’s a playmaker. Just a great player.”

On the ensuing Barnstable drive, Burke hit Bo Delaney on a deep post for a 35-yard completion and first down at the Dolphins’ 16. After struggling with Barnstable’s bullish offensive line most of the game, Funk dialed up some outside pressure and let senior L.K. Metz run loose, coming up with a crucial sack on third down with frontside pressure from outside the tackle box.

The ensuing pass on fourth down fell short, and the Dolphins ran out the final 2:30 to ice the win.

Jamiel finished the night with nine catches for 48 yards, and five carries for 63 yards and a score.

For Barnstable, regarded as one of the best home field advantages in Massachusetts, dropped just its second home contest since 2010. Two years ago at this same field, they cruised to a 55-14 blitzkrieg of the Dolphins, kick-starting a thrilling run to the Division 1A Super Bowl in which they spent seven weeks ranked as the No. 1 team in the state. Last year, the Dolphins returned the favor with a 39-9 rout, kick-starting a run to the inaugural Division 4 State championship.

Buying Burke time: It wasn’t until late in the contest that the Dolphins finally broke through Barnstable’s pass protection, sending Metz off the edge to hurry Burke into making a decision. But for the most of the night, the outsized Dolphins had a tough time penetrating the Raiders’ offensive line.

Six-foot-4, 250-pound left tackle Owen Murray gets most of the accolades in the trenches, and rightfully so after a bright junior season battling some of the region’s top defensive linemen. But between guards Ben Capra and Donny Mach, center Colton Bergal and right tackle Tanner Banks, this was a solid unit up and down.

Under fourth-year head coach Chris Whidden, Barnstable has been known for its vertical passing game, stretching the secondary deep downfield with an array of streaks and posts. The most telling stat for the Raiders’ pass protection tonight is that Burke, a load at 6-foot-3 and 225 pounds, completed just 6 of 17 passes. But he totaled 167 yards on those six completions, with all but one pass – a seven-yard back-pylon strike that closed D-Y’s lead to 28-21 with 5:43 to go – went for 32 yards or more.

In other words, Burke had a lot of time to pick his precise target.

“That offensive line is unbelievable. We had no answer for them,” Funk said. “The kid [Burke] had about eight seconds to throw, so I have to give them credit.”

Their bulk in the trenches carried into the running game, where senior tailback Demichael Johnson had a breakout performance. Johnson finished with 19 carries for 130 yards, including four rushes over 15 yards, at times getting wide lines to accelerate through.

Crosby’s Show: While the dominant thought with D-Y headed into the season was that jack-of-all-trades athlete Michael Dunn would slide into a more permanent quarterbacking role this year after scoring from nearly every position last fall, tonight played out differently.

Of D-Y’s 53 offensive snaps tonight, Dunn lined up under center on 23 of them, and played tailback on 12 others (He missed 17 snaps in the second half with calf cramps).

Ian Crosby, a wiry 5-foot-11 junior, took the rest of the snaps, at times deploying both him and Dunn in a read option game. Of all the revelations tonight, Crosby was the most pleasant, completing six straight passes to start the night and finishing 10 for 15. His primary benefactor was Jamiel, getting the elusive senior going on an array of swing passes and tunnel screens.

“I told you guys earlier [in the preseason], don’t be surprised if Ian takes snaps at quarterback,” Funk said. “The kid is a competitor. He’s an old-fashioned football player. He just competes. Nothing ever looks pretty, and it’s no offense to him, but he just competes and we like what he does.”

Recap: Oliver Ames 21, No. 19 Plymouth North 7

September, 5, 2014
Sep 5
11:23
PM ET
By Ryan Lanigan | ESPNBoston.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0
  • Comments0
  • Email
  • Print
NORTH EASTON, Mass. -- Oliver Ames' offense showed some bursts in the first three quarters of the game, but couldn't put it all together.

That changed completely in the final frame as the Tigers erupted for three touchdowns in the final nine minutes of play to beat No. 19 Plymouth North, 21-7.

While OA's offense worked out the kinks, their defense was able to shut down anything Plymouth North's offense threw at them. The Eagles, who were without star running back Christian Carr because of a knee injury, also looked to be trying to figure things out offensively.

"I think we're different than we were last year," Oliver Ames head coach Jim Artz said of his defense. "I don't think we have some of the size we had but I think our team speed is very good. I think we can fly to the ball and we don't overflow sometimes, and I think they hurt us with that with some very nice cut back runs."

After a scoreless first half, the first breakthrough came through less than a minute into the second half. On Oliver Ames' second play of the half, a muffed handoff resulted in a fumble that was recovered by Plymouth North's Justin Cabral at the OA 23-yard line. Plymouth North's Myles Uva (12 carries, 59 yards) took the first play 17 yards on a run to the right and then took the next carry six yards and into the end zone to put the Eagles up 7-0.

OA and Plymouth North each traded punts before the Tigers took over at their own 25-yard line. Faced with third and six at the Eagles' 22-yard line, OA quarterback John McMillan hit junior receiver Franklin Holgate on a short pass but Holgate chugged his way through the defense and dove over the 15-yard line for a gain of eight and a first down. On the next play, senior Michael Ferrara rushed it straight up the middle for a 14-yard touchdown to tie the game, 7-7.

Plymouth North was forced into a three in out on the next possession, mainly because of a shoestring tackle from Oliver Ames linebacker Joey Tepper. On third and seven from their own 29-yard line, Plymouth North quarterback Cullin Cosgrove dropped very deep, drawing in the defensive line, before dumping it off to Uva on a screen pass. It looked like Uva had plenty of space but Tepper quickly closed down and tripped him up before the first down marker.

"He's good," Artz said of Tepper. "He's very, very good. I thought the play defensively for us in the game was that screen he sniffs out. He tracked the kid down, tripped the kid up and makes an unbelievable play. Then I think he ran a 4.3 off the field."

That play forced Plymouth North to punt and eight plays later, Holgate was at it again. McMillan hit Holgate for another quick pass and this time Holgate broke through the first tackle and then sprinted up the sideline for a 43-yard touchdown. Steven Scurti's extra point was good to make it 14-7 Oliver Ames with 2:43 left.

As the Eagles tried to conduct a game-tying drive, the Tigers brought the pressure. Cosgrove was flushed from the pocket and OA seniors Dan Sullivan and Patrick McNeely closed in and forced a fumble. Sophomore Shawn Cardoza recovered the fumble at the Eagles' 40-yard line and OA took over with 1:24 let.

After one rushing play went for two yards and Plymouth North called timeout, the Tigers went play action and McMillan found a wide open Sam Kelly along the left sidelines for a 38-yard touchdown, giving OA a 21-7 lead.

"They found some seams and made some plays," Plymouth North head coach Kevin Cobban said. "They made two more plays than we did. There was a big penalty early on when we could have gone up 7-0, that could have changed things a little bit. For the first game of the year, I thought it was a pretty good football game. We know what we have to work on and we'll get better. We have nothing to hang our heads about, they're a good team and we're a good team."

McMillan, who started his first varsity game, looked a lot more comfortable in the second half for the Tigers. He went 4-8 for 32 yards in the first half but went 10-11 for 125 yards and two touchdowns in the second half.

"I think it's a comfort level," Artz said of throwing more later in the game. "With a new quarterback, I kind of have to get that feel. They're very good up front, it's not like they gave us a ton of time either. That was a very strong, very good physical football team. We had to pick our moments and not get too greedy and make a costly mistake. Defensively if we make them drive the whole field, we're very tough."

As for Carr, Cobban said that he's getting an MRI this week.
