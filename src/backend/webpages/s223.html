Cutler, Bears' offense must be better

Chicago QB should own up to mistakes -- like his fourth-quarter INT against Buffalo

By Michael Wilbon | ESPNChicago.com

CHICAGO -- It's about the worst scenario imaginable one game into a new and hopeful season, the Chicago Bears losing at home to the long-irrelevant Buffalo Bills, the run defense continuing to be historically bad, Alshon Jeffery suffering a potentially ominous hamstring injury, and worst of all, Jay Cutler throwing a hideous fourth-quarter interception, then directing his public anger at everybody but himself. And yes, the scariest part of it once again is Cutler, apparently unchanged by his $100 million contract or status. Cutler and coach Marc Trestman, immediately after the game, had convinced themselves and tried to convince those of us listening to them postgame that "lots of good things" happened for the Bears against the Bills. Now, you can call this staying on message or spinning ... whatever you want. 

[+] Enlarge
AP Photo/Nam Y. HuhJay Cutler and Marc Trestman said postgame that "lots of good things" happened for the Bears against the Bills -- but that's not enough for a team built to win now.

It's garbage.

The Bears aren't Theo's Cubs. They're not a bunch of kids just promoted to the big leagues, playing for three years from now. The Bears, particularly this offense, are built to win games now. Even when the defense and special teams stink, which seems to be inevitable, this offense is built to score points now ... like 35 a game. Not seven in the first half, not 20 at home on a perfect 75-degree day for pitch-and-catch. Twenty points for a team with Cutler/Matt Forte/Jeffery/Brandon Marshall/Martellus Bennett is about a half-day's work against a team like the Bills, who major in going 6-10. 

Nothing good of any real consequence happened if you lost at home to the Bills, when six of your next eight games are on the road, including at the 49ers, Patriots and Packers. 

The worst of it was the pick Cutler threw in the fourth quarter on third-and-1 from his own 34 with the game tied at 17. It really was a nice-looking play, Cutler faking everybody left and rolling to his right to create time and space. Except he then fired back into traffic and was picked off by Kyle Williams, a defensive tackle. Seriously, it was a terrible decision -- awful, vintage Bad Cutler for way too many years now. Apparently, no amount of guaranteed money can buy the Bears out of having to see Cutler make just that throw with the game on the line. 

Still, it happens.

But then Cutler did what is infinitely more infuriating than throwing the pick. He went surly because somebody dared ask him why he threw a really dumb pass with the game on the line. Instead of just saying, "I threw a stupid pass. It's my fault," which would have diffused the frustration as it just about always does, Cutler shot back with obvious annoyance, "You guys are going to be as negative as possible. ... No one [among the media] knows exactly what we're doing out there. You guys think you do, but you don't."

Here's what I damn sure know. When you're paid face-of-the-franchise money, you publicly cop to the same dumb interception you've thrown 20 times, even if it wasn't your fault this time. And there's no pat on the back coming because you threw for 349 yards and a couple of touchdowns. Good things happened? Do he and Trestman want a trophy and orange slices like they're my 6-year-old playing Saturday mornings?

Not enough good things happened.

You're a team with aspirations that lost to Buffalo at home.

I don't care to know the specifics that Cutler bristled about. I do know he has made similar awful intercepted throws more than once with the game on the line. I do know that men who know the specifics of what Cutler is trying to do better than he does -- like the former quarterbacks and coaches (some of them Hall of Famers) who make their living as professional analysts -- say on-the-record that it's exactly those kinds of throws that have characterized Cutler's play, and will continue to haunt him and the Bears.

Look, there's plenty of time for the Bears to go to work and get better, and clearly Mel Tucker's unit has to, because allowing 193 yards rushing -- the defense made C.J. look like O.J. -- is a season-killer if it keeps happening. The Bears were dead last, 32nd, in the league in run defense last year and looked every bit as incompetent Sunday.

But if you told me the defense would have allowed 20 points by the end of regulation, I'd have bet on a Bears victory. The offense is supposed to be more than good enough to cover that, even with Jeffery off the field in the fourth quarter, even with Marshall hobbling, even with two linemen forced off the field by sprained ankles. There's no excuse for an offense with the weapons and depth the Bears had entering the game to score seven lousy points in the first half. None. And Cutler being testy with postgame questions doesn't change that one bit.

More than ever the NFL is a quarterback league. The rules have (again) been tweaked to allow the top throwers to dominate the game. And Cutler, as we've heard since his days of saying he has a stronger arm than John Elway had, is supposed to be a top thrower. He's damn sure paid like one.

[+] Enlarge
David Banks/Getty ImagesAsked about his costly interception in the fourth quarter, Jay Cutler snapped at the media instead of just taking responsibility for the bad throw.

The offensive line, as Cutler pointed out, played pretty darn well despite juggling personnel with Roberto Garza and Matt Slauson out. Cutler is surrounded by playmakers. The coach-and-quarterback marriage seems to be a good one, and Cutler indeed has the talent to make the whole thing go. Yes, there are going to be interceptions and fumbles and penalties (the Bears  had only four in the opener, hardly a sign of great carelessness). Still, the offense is and will be the strength of this team for the foreseeable future, and it's that unit's job to do the heavy lifting -- at least for the first half of this season, if not the entire season. 

So, as Chris Rock says, you don't deserve lavish praise for doing what you're supposed to do. It doesn't matter that the offense ran up 29 first downs to Buffalo's 15, or that the Bears won the time-of-possession battle, or that Cutler went 34-for-49 for 349 yards and two touchdowns. This offense is supposed to do that. And against the Buffalos of the world -- and there simply aren't many patsies on the Bears schedule considering the way the Vikings, Dolphins, Panthers and Falcons looked in Week 1 -- this offense has to do enough to win, and it didn't. The three undermined everything.

The coach and quarterback talked about "cleaning up" the mistakes, the turnovers and penalties that sabotaged the ball movement that resulted in 427 net yards of total offense. Well, they'd better get a big mop and get back to work because the next opponent is so much more formidable than the Bills. The Niners are next up and they've made a habit of eating the Bears for lunch every time the Monsters fly west. If C.J. Spiller, Fred Jackson and Anthony Dixon run over, past and through the Bears for nearly 200 yards, what in the world do you think Frank Gore/Carlos Hyde/Colin Kaepernick are capable of doing at home?

The point is, the Bears' best hope next week in California is that Marshall/Jeffery/Slauson/Garza are pretty close to fully healthy, and that they can engage in a shootout. Twenty points ain't gonna get it done. Neither will drive-killing decisions and venting after losses that the media just doesn't understand. That will come off as it always does: Unbelievably lame, and it's too early in the season to conclude that Trestman, Cutler and the members of this offense can't do any better than this.
