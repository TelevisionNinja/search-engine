USC Trojans

Andre Heidari, J.R. Tavai change the tone for Trojans

September, 7, 2014
Sep 7
7:28
PM PT
By Steve Bisheff | ESPN.com
  • Recommend0
  • Tweet0

Andre Heidari's dramatic 53-yard field goal and J.R. Tavai's stadium-rattling sack weren't just game-changers for USC on Saturday.

They were tone-changers. The whole tone of the Trojans' football season changed with that gritty 13-10 victory over Stanford in Palo Alto.

Before, Steve Sarkisian's kids were the feisty outsiders trying to squeeze into the national picture. Not anymore. Suddenly, a new, confident USC is within the Top 10, looking as if it will be favored in every game on its schedule leading up to the Nov. 22 crosstown duel with UCLA. And based on how the Bruins have looked in their first two wobbly games, who knows which team will be favored in that one.

[+] Enlarge
Cary Edmondson/USA TODAY SportsAndre Heidari has connected on big kicks to beat Stanford both of the past two seasons.
It's all happened because of a wild, controversial afternoon in the Bay Area that had everyone scratching their heads afterward. How exactly did the Trojans do it after being outgained 413 yards to 291 and having fewer first downs (21 vs. 16)?

And why did Sarkisian, A) go all conservative on offense, running on third-and-longs and throwing basically to only one receiver, and B) how in the name of John McKay did he completely blow his cool with the referees, costing his team a 15-yard unsportsmanlike penalty and then calling down athletic director Pat Haden from the press box?

And what was Haden thinking actually running onto the field? Sure, he wanted to help his team, but there is a matter of football decorum here, and Haden flagrantly ignored it.

Somehow, USC overcame all that by simply converting big plays when it needed them most, something a straining Stanford couldn't do. The Cardinal muscled down inside the Trojans' 30-yard line nine times and manufactured only 10 points from all those opportunities.

The Trojans won because Heidari made the kick of his life, Tavai produced a huge sack and Leonard Williams and Javorius “Buck” Allen played like serious All-American candidates.

Heidari has kicked Stanford into submission twice now, but his range always has seemed somewhat, uh, limited. How many USC boosters out there really believed he'd convert that 53-yarder? Let's just say they probably weren't in the majority.

Tavai's sack that produced the Kevin Hogan fumble was appropriate, because this has long been one of the more underrated athletes on the Trojans' roster. Whenever he's in the game, he makes things happen, even if he's never been deemed good enough to start.

Williams' sore ankle made him questionable for the game, but he sucked it up and continued to play like one of the finest defensive tackles in the school's storied history. As for Allen, well, it's time for everyone to recognize this guy is the real deal. He has great feet, wonderful vision and runs with the type of smooth, gliding motion reminiscent of another Allen at USC named Marcus.

As stirring as this victory was, it was also confusing based on how the Trojans played the previous week against Fresno State. They were so much more aggressive offensively and defensively in the opener.

Although it's impossible to argue with the result, you can't help but wonder why Sarkisian changed his strategy so completely for Stanford. What happened to spreading the ball around to all those athletic receivers? Sark was almost Kiffin-esque in his approach to this game, throwing everything to one guy, Nelson Agholor, the way USC did a year ago with Marqise Lee.

Justin Wilcox's defense changed, too, failing to blitz much until the end, playing what almost seemed like a Monte Kiffin-like Tampa 2 defense much of the time, allowing Stanford's Hogan to pick it apart with passes down the middle.

Maybe Sark and Wilcox knew it would be a close, low-scoring game. The oddsmakers sure didn't. The over-under point total going in was 55.

Happily, USC won't be facing another big, physical opponent again until late November against Notre Dame.

That should mean a more relaxed, free-wheeling approach in coming weeks, one that should be more successful on the scoreboard and more fun to watch.

Whatever this coaching staff decided to do strategically against Stanford, the bottom line is that it somehow worked. In the clutch, Heidari, Tavai and USC did what it had to do.

The whole Trojans' season just swerved in time to navigate past its biggest early obstacle. And the ride the rest of the way suddenly looks like it might be smoother than anyone imagined.
